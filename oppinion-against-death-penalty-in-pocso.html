Opinion : Against Death Penalty in POCSO

  •   Sat 21 April 2018
  •   Psychology
  •   psychology law justice india crime behaviour

Today the union cabinet approved an ordinance prescribing the death penalty for rape of girls below age of twelve. The central government had submitted to the supreme court that the POCSO (Protection of Children from Sexual Offences) act shall be amended to make punishments more stringent in cases of rape against minors below 16 and 12 years of age. The ordinance approved today increases the punishment from 10 years to 20 years extendable to life imprisonment for rape of girls below age of 16 and the ability to award death penalty in cases of rape of girls below 12. Union Cabinet Approves Ordinance for death penalty for rape of girls under 12 years (2019, April,2018) Retrieved from: livelaw.in

While a widely demanded change in view of the increasing crimes against children in the country, the implications of this reaction to the current rape incidents can harm the delivery of justice and weaken the POCSO act itself. What may become the biggest pain going forward with such a law in action might not come from legal challenges or technical difficulties but shall arise from the very core of human behaviour.

Challenges

Makes The Decision Tougher To Make

It is a well established fact that humans do not like to execute actions that can adversely affect others around them if possible. This is evident even in rating inflation of services; such as when a person leave a five star rating to an Uber driver even though his service was not adequate. People do this as the cost such an action, which could be guilt or the fear of retaliation. These can easily outweigh the need to act (Filipas, Horton and Golden, 2018).

With such a heavy penalty hanging over the subject, the judge, prosecutor and investigator can become even more sensitive to the actions they take. The question in power here would be if they would take that call that may put a person in death row. While some may become extremely cautious in selection, charging and disposal of the cases, some others may opt to totally avoid making that choice.

With hundreds to thousands of people involved in our criminal justice system, and each of them being individuals with their own philosophies and values, we run the risk of the question shifting from "should we not get justice for the victim" to "Should we put a mans life on the line". This could be a very undesirable situation.

Judges shall become even more cautious

The stress involved in making a decision is impacted majorly by the implications of the said decision ( Geoffrey Churchill and Thomas Whalen, 2002). The stress involved in deciding on life of a person would be phenomenal. This would mean the person entrusted to take the decision becomes more cautious when making the decision. The weight of the decision shall also demand more certainty regarding the information that the decision is based on.

In a criminal trial this would add additional pressure on the prosecution and investigating officers to build and present an air tight case. If they fail to do the same judge may decide to acquit or award a small sentence as this is an easier decision compared to the delivery of death penalty.

A judge who previously took decision based on eyewitness accounts and smaller set of evidence may now prefer to depend on stronger evidence including forensics which our system may not yet be ready to deliver on.

Poor performing criminal justice system in conflict with the judiciary

To understand the implication of death penalty it is critical we understand the existing statistics of death penalty cases and the opinion of judges involved. Out of 60 former supreme court justices interviewed 47 had decided on death penalty cases and most did not see and issue with it. However their view of the criminal justice system was very different. 43 out of the 60 interviewed stressed that wrongful convictions are a worrying reality. The problems they saw with the criminal justice system also included torture and fabrication of evidence. National Law University Delhi, (2017)

Add to this the fact that 95% of death sentences awarded by trial courts are thrown out by higher courts (National Law University New Delhi,2016). Even more interesting is the fact that 29.87% of the 1486 death penalties awarded were acquitted. The idea of sending an innocent man to death row in a society where criminal laws are often misued for personal gains or vendetta is disturbing enough.

This sort of a situation means that the judiciary can't trust the criminal justice system, and to base a decision of life of a citizen on such an unreliable system would by itself be a stress factor for judges making the decision. The unreliable system will also overload the judiciary with the duty of having to fact check and fix the mistakes made by it, costing time and effort.

Weakens POCSO

One of the strongest provisions of the POCSO act is considered to be that the burden of proving innocence is on the accused. With the death penalty involved now, no judge in effect can use this mechanism confidently as it may strike both ethical and empathetic lines in presuming a person guilty of a crime that can be punished with death. The time old innocent until proven guilty shall prevail.

This by default removes one of the strongest arm designed into POCSO hence weakening it from the core.

Conclusion

Human decision making is a very complex one. By involving a controversial provision of death penalty into a much required and actively used law has burdened the judicial and other systems of having to make a tougher and inefficient decision than a faster and efficient one. This can not only cripple the effective use of the law as even slight technicalities can cause smaller trials or acquittals due to the weight of decision. Instead of faster and better delivery we may be plagued by slower and ineffective one.

The current situation is such that either the accused shal walk away with smaller sentances or be acquited altogether.I am of the opinion that longer sentences would have been a much more effective solution in this case due to the above facts.
